A Simplified Style Guide for MTU Papers

A best practices guide for those tasked with writing a Monthly Technology Update

Version: 1.0.0

Introduction

The Monthly Technology Update (MTU) is a series of papers written by team leads under Mark Chamberlain's OUT structure. Every TL that reports under Mark will at some time be tasked with writing a paper on a technology specific to, and originated within that group. This is an effort to help TLs write the most optimal paper that best represents themselves, their group, and Mark's organization.

This is a minimalist style guide that intends to lay out the most basic outlines of style for a MTU or similar paper. The approach here is not to replace a full style or grammar guide, but to assist in the most common issues that MTU writers deal with.

The primary value of this paper is to increase effective communication between groups and to foster creative & effective engineering.

Discussion Points

What should the paper be about?

A primary goal of Mark's organization is to engineer solutions that give us a competitive edge and/or advance our capabilities. Any technical design or engineered solution that contributes to this goal are very good candidates for things to discuss.

When is it due?

The month's MTU is due on the Monday of the last full work-week in the prior month. So if October ends on a Tuesday, November's paper is due on the second-to-last Monday of October. Parsing the logic can be a bit of a pain, so the schedule and dates for the current rotation can be found on the MTU page.

The paper is due before the first of the month to allow time for proof reading, posting to TEAM, and the announcement on (or near) the first of the Month.

What file format should I use?

The MTU is hosted from a Wiki/TEAM page, but does not designate one format as being required over another. There are a number of popular formats with each having pros and cons. The three formats that are most appropriate for authoring are HTML, MS Word, and Wiki/TEAM. PDF is included as a distribution format. LaTeX is included as an authoring/formatting format primarily for comparison, but also because much of the "custom" CSS used in this document is based on LaTeX commands.

How long should it be?

It has been argued that a target length should be established before the paper is started. The thinking is that boundaries such as this keep the writer on task and results in a better paper.

While a technical paper should have some limits on length, the stronger advice is to use the space (verbiage) required to relate your points as clearly as possible and no more. The primary goal is to relate the technical issues set forth in the task. The secondary goal is to keep the paper short enough so that the reader will likely read it all.

Who should write it?

The paper is assigned to TLs, not groups. The paper is therefore a reflection upon the TL. Should the TL delegate it to a team member, it is appropriate for the TL to give proper credit for authorship but also remember that the paper is still a reflection upon the TL it was originally assigned.

Does the paper require approval from William?

No. William collects the papers and facilitates publishing. William also offers basic proof reading with the intent of presenting the paper, the TL, the team, and the technology in the best light.

Who is the target audience?

Papers should be written to a target audience. Good writing generally appeals to a wide audience. While technical papers cannot appeal to the same wide audiences as fiction, they can similarly benefit from a wider appeal. Here is a sample list of who you should be writing to in your paper:

Yourself (past) When you were discovering this subject you struggled with imprecise descriptions, vague definitions, and overly wordy text. You had to weed through lots of poor writing and various sources to finally come to the perfected understanding you now possess. Write the things that you wish you had in the beginning of the journey. If someone said it better, then quote or reference (specifically) the parts that help.
Yourself (future) Write to a future you that is likely to forget key details. You understand the subject, but will want to come back for the specifics of a formula or a key piece of info that is harder to get somewhere else. Here you are looking to capture hard points of data that you have recognized as valuable based on your experience. Simply writing that item X is settable in "the config file" is a lazy approach that future-you will not readily appreciate. You may wish to relate something like: "X is settable through the VALUE_X attribute where X is expressed in thousands of a second (as opposed to the standard percentages used elsewhere). When setting X, we found that the proper baseline was Y, and increments of Z produce the rate of change we desired for this effort."
Technically familiar There are people who understand or have direct experience with most of the concepts of your paper. The paper should not seem remedial or bore them. It has to have appeal (value) to those who have some background in the subject. The primary means of reaching these people is to explain details or insights into the subject that they are likely to have missed. Another is to expound on some of the less-technical design considerations chosen that may differ from one implementation to another.
Technically distant Some people will consider your paper very foreign. They will not have substantial experience in your domain. It is important that the paper not make assumptions about what they know. For this reason it is important to properly frame the discussion with a ramping introduction, clear context, and real world examples. Another method to reach these people is to put items in general context. For example to relate context beyond the specific technical data one might include a means for people not as familiar to understand the significance of the stats that might not make immediate sense to them: "The frackulator achieved 300 clecks/second - that represents a 37% improvement in efficiency that saved five minutes off processing time.".

What are some of the common pitfalls?

Lists Be mindful of the context of lists. Is the list a way of emphasis of a terms and a conjunction? If so, then the list should have an introduction like a sentence clause and likely not have punctuation (although you can think of each line ending in a comma). Is the list a series of tasks? Then it should be ordered (numbered), be complete sentences, and should have full punctuation.
Grammar Bad grammar is simple to fix. MS Word or just a second set of eyes can knock down the most obvious grammatical errors. Excuses should not be made for bad grammar - You are not James Joyce, you are a technical writer trying to best represent yourself and your points.
Voice Papers should speak in an active voice.3 Active voice is more about precision, is more engaging to the reader, and more appropriate for a MTU. An example of this might be: "The service was then put into production.", as opposed to a more active voiced version that says "Our group then placed the service into production."
Personal pronouns Technical papers can have a tendency to waver in the use of personal pronouns. No absolute position is taken here other than to be consistent and mindful in their usage. That said, considering that the paper is authored by the TL in regards to the team he leads, then first person plural (we, us, our) should, in most cases, be the predominant form in the document.
Consistency Many of the "rules" here do not have absolute answers. What works best, regardless of the side of a style argument you land on is that you consistently apply that style throughout the document. For example; issues like the gender-neutral replacement for "he" can fall into several different camps. In this case it is not as important that one or the other be used, but whatever choice is made be applied consistently.
Terms & acronyms When introducing new terms, a description of that term should be included on the first inclusion. This can be inline, via footnote, URL or other means. It is a problem for your reader to continue reading without understanding a term that is used in your presentation. Acronyms should be treated in a similar manner; they should be introduced first in expanded form with the acronym in parens.4 For example: "This document is heavily reliant on Three Letter Acronyms (TLAs)." This is about domain specific, vague, and multi-definition terms. What TCP is about should be understood by most grandmothers at this point. But, by comparison, terms seemingly specific as "cache" could refer to many different things. The usage context of the term may be sufficient to carry the reader, but when in doubt, always include some assistance for your audience.

[4. This is the method espoused in the Chicago Manual of Style. (15th ed. section 15.2) It is considered acceptable for this to be reversed. ]

What are some simple wins for adding value?

Different people will view your document differently. Each will look for understanding and a final meaning through a different means. If you are only speaking one way, you stand to subtly alienate different types from your desired end-result of satisfied readers.

Logical diagrams Diagrams explain relationships between the items you are discussing with representations that visually infer what you say. Many people are visually/spatially oriented and absorb information better through an accompanying image that speaks more clearly (to them) than words. When used in conjunction with words, you reach a wider audience.
External references Others have spent a large amount of time to best relate various concepts, and may have perfected the message more than you can hope to in the time you have allotted. It is not plagiarism or taking an unfair shortcut to reference their work.
Quantitative data Managers will want to see items such as: "how much time was saved", and "how much did this effort cost". Some of your readers will not be looking at specifics of what was done, but what it accomplished and in terms that they understand.
Examples Like the previously mentioned visual thinkers, some people will want to see how the technology relates to "real world" process. These people will be looking for how this technology changed a process they know and/or how they can directly use this technology themselves.
Code snippets Those comfortable with the concepts may want to peer deeper into implementation details. Those well versed in various languages may wish to just see exactly what was done. In these cases, code speaks louder than words.
BNF BNF (Backus–Naur Form) or its derivatives can be used to definitively relate what might be a fairly complex description if relying upon sentence-paragraph structure alone.
Footnotes Footnotes serve multiple purposes. The obvious use in technical papers is to include a reference. But they can also serve as an opportunity to push equivocation off to a side conversation. Your document can use a more flowing conversation without stopping to handle exceptions and nuances that are issues, but not related directly to your point.
Q-n-A A question and answer section allows the writer to handle lots of ad-hoc items that may not be as easy to approach in other parts of the document. The writer can use this opportunity to directly address salient points in a direct response format. Successive follow-on question-response exchanges can form a dialectic that forms a greater argument.
Glossary While it is unlikely that a single paper would contain sufficient content to necessitate a glossary, it is not out of the question that a paper could have one. Combining terms into one section may help to draw a distinction between subtle differences that may be lost in the inline introduction of the terms. Definitions can also be placed inline. For example: individual definitions can be set apart in an indented / boxed style throughout the text where they are most topically relevant.

While the above items can each add value to a document, they are not a checklist of items that must be included. Each additional input to the effort can have a tendency to add an additional "weight" in terms of value, but also in terms of complexity. The goal should be to balance the value of the additions against their disruptive complexity.

What are the best ways for including non-text into the document?

The term "non-text items" refers primarily to diagrams, code blocks, and images, but can also include alternatives such as embedded videos or spreadsheet data. Including this information can provide a quick win in terms of relating a subject to the reader, but it can also wreak havoc on the readability of the document if it becomes too pervasive. Finding a balance and the best way to stylistically include the content is of high importance.

The following is a sample of code that has been inserted into the document. Including code in HTML can be unpleasant and time consuming when done by hand. The following example has no syntax highlighting, but has syntactically non-valid comments in a clearly different color. Syntax highlighting would make the code more readable, but subtract from the ability to call out individual lines as is done here. #include <stdio.h>

int main(int argc, char *argv[])
{
   printf("Hello World!\n");  <— The magic sauce in this app is here
   return(0);

Images can be very useful in relating points. Logical diagrams and screen shots have a tendency to bring the user to understanding faster than trying to relate the same information by words alone. So the recommendation is to use images when they serve to make things clearer.

When using images, the recommendation is to use them sparingly in your document and use them as a way to reinforce the point of your text. When too many images are included, they have a tendency to overwhelm the text and make readability more difficult. When a series of images are required, then there are a number of stylistic methods that can be used such as keeping a consistent size and placement for successive images so that they are less disruptive to the visual flow of the document.

Does the font matter?

The fundamental visual appeal of the document matters. This includes choices as seemingly simple as font choices, but also formatting and page layout options. Serifed fonts improve readability of bodies of text, while sans-serif versions may have issues distinguishing characters such as 1, l, and I.7

So you think you are done

You have written your paper, it looks good. What should you do now? Turning it in will remove the task from your plate, but is the paper really reflective on how you want to be perceived? Does the paper actually reach the full audience that it will be sent to? I have included a list of some things that you can do to potentially improve upon the final product.

Ask yourself: "Was the paper easy to write?"

The effort put into the paper is a fair indicator of the value of the paper. For example:

• Did you take the time to look up key details? Did you have to go back and look at the code? Writing about items from the top of your memory tends to speak to overview details, and frequently misses specifics.
• Did you take the time to capture the largest possible audience possible? It takes effort to anticipate what different members of your audience are looking for and to try to satisfy those needs.
• Did your content provide more than the outline view? The muscle that hangs off of the skeletal structure (the outline) is the value that makes the document. Without this muscle, the document lacks the appeal you desire.

Walk away

Take a break from the paper, and do something else. Give yourself some distance from the initial effort, and then come back. When you come back, give the paper a critical read. You should look to see if the document flows like you thought it did. You should look to see if the paper provides the impression you thought it did. This is a bit of "future you" looking at your work.

Have someone proofread your work

Your paper will be announced to an initial set of about 150 readers but published to all of TEAM. Some of those readers will be your managers, some will be peers, and some will be those that answer to you. These people will be reading with a critical eye. So it will be read and critiqued. It is in your best interests that you let someone with an equally critical eye read the paper before you push it to TEAM and a much larger audience.

Defining Success

When the paper is published, you want to hear: "Wow, I did not know that."
